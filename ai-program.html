My Learning Journey

Summary · 學習總結

From Trial-and-Error
to Structured Thinking

From trial-and-error to structured thinking — learning how to truly work with AI.

My journey into AI began as a self-driven experiment — trying to build machine learning models in Colab during the winter break, often facing frustration and misalignment when working with AI tools.

Over time, especially after transitioning to VS Code and collaborating with GPT and Claude, I started to move beyond trial-and-error and develop a more structured way of thinking. AI became not just a tool, but a system to align with.

Through coursework and project presentations, including my text mining midterm, I learned how to organize ideas, communicate clearly, and reflect on both my own performance and the shared experience of my peers.

I also became more aware of the reality around me — balancing study, work, and fatigue — and began to see learning not only as technical progress, but as a long-term process of persistence.

While the changes may seem small, I have built stronger logical thinking and project structuring skills. More importantly, I have learned how to work with AI effectively.

This journey is still ongoing, but it has already reshaped how I approach problems, learning, and growth.

Learning Journal · 學習日誌
— Act I · Beginning —

1. The Beginning — A Spark

I first stepped into the AI world in March 2025, during a two-day AI Application Planner course. What stayed with me the most was not the content itself, but a teacher whose passion for AI deeply moved me. That moment planted a seed. Later, I even had the opportunity to become part of his graduation project team. It felt like a quiet beginning of something meaningful.

2. Feeling Small in a Big Room

At the freshman orientation, I saw classmates from all kinds of professional backgrounds. I felt small and unsure of myself. Terms like Decision Trees and SVM sounded distant and unfamiliar. I barely understood what was being discussed, yet I knew one thing clearly — I wanted to take something real away from this AI program.

3. Confusion and Overload

Every day except Monday, there were different AI classes taught by different instructors. Each brought new knowledge, new perspectives, and new complexity. I often found myself in a strange state — half understanding, half lost. It was overwhelming, yet I kept going.

— Act II · Struggle —

4. Sacrifice and Exhaustion

Between work and school, my life became extremely compressed. Apart from walking fifteen minutes from the MRT station to the classroom, I had almost no time left for myself — not even for exercise. The Python course was especially intense. There were moments when I genuinely questioned whether I had chosen the wrong path.

5. A Familiar Struggle

When deadlines for two statistics courses approached, I was suddenly pulled back to memories of writing my master's thesis in accounting at Fu Jen University. The feeling was both familiar and distant — a mix of pressure, discipline, and quiet persistence.

6. Fighting to Work with AI

During the first semester, working with AI tools was not easy. I wanted to collaborate with them effectively, but reality was far from ideal. I spent a significant amount of time trying to complete projects, yet often felt limited by my own abilities. Still, I moved forward quietly and finished the semester.

7. Struggling Alone in Winter

During the winter break, I tried to build machine learning models on my own using Colab. It was a process of trial and error — almost like crafting something from scratch without guidance. I invested a lot of time and effort, but kept getting stuck, especially in the gap between myself and effective AI collaboration.

— Act III · Turning Point —

8. A Turning Point — Alignment

Everything started to change after the new semester began. Working in VS Code with GPT and Claude, I finally achieved alignment in how to collaborate with AI. From that moment on, something shifted. Every project and every topic I cared about became more manageable. Both the speed and quality of my work came under control.

9. Looking Back — Nothing Was Wasted

I still remember what Nick once told me — that none of my struggles were wasted. Looking back now, I understand what he meant. Every obstacle I faced became part of my foundation. Those moments of being stuck were not failures; they were preparation.

— Act IV · Now —

10. Where I Am Now

Now, with about two months left before the semester ends, I can see that I have changed — even if only a little. I have gained stronger logical thinking and a better sense of project structure. Today, April 18, 2026, I attended an online class at home. After pushing myself to stay awake despite exhaustion, I finally gave in and fell asleep. I woke up at 6 PM to find food.

11. Gratitude and Reality

I am grateful to my body for carrying me through this journey. In some ways, not much has changed — I even gained some weight. But I have learned something about AI, and that matters. More importantly, I am still here, still moving forward.

12. Looking Ahead

What I look forward to now is June — the end of the semester, and a trip to Japan. It feels like a small reward waiting at the end of a long and quiet journey.
